Project Snapshot

Managing expenses is tough for many Millennials (born 1981 – 1996) and Gen Z (born 1997 – 2012). Most finance apps are either too complex or missing essential features which leave users stressed and struggling to stay on top of their money management.

XpensePal is a mobile app concept designed to make budgeting and saving simple, while also supporting long-term financial goals.

Research & Discovery

I began by uncovering user frustrations through secondary research like reports and competitive analysis, then defined key pain points before iterating on solutions and testing with users.

I looked at a 2023 TIAA Institute and GFLEC study, which revealed critical gaps that XpensePal could address. For instance,

  • Knowledge Gaps: Low scores in financial literacy suggest apps should embed financial education in plain language, not assume users already know
  • Savings & Emergencies: The majority of Gen Z and Millennials can’t handle financial shocks, which highlights the need for proactive savings nudges.
  • Day-to-Day Struggles: With debt limiting priorities and users spending 10+ hours a week on finances, there’s a clear demand for simplification and automation.

User Research

I conducted 30 surveys (mix of Gen Z and Gen Y), which resulted in 8 follow-up user interviews.

  • Confusing login flow with redundant steps
  • Lack of guidance for first-time users
  • No quick or easy support when users get stuck

These insights helped me zero in on the key issues to address in the redesign.

Design question

How might I create a finance app that feels approachable and supportive while helping users build healthier financial habits?

Discovery & Research

I used the Double Diamond process with a Design Thnking mindset

Competitive Analysis

I reviewed existing apps and identified gaps around onboarding, budgeting flexibility, and education

Then, I interviewed three Cratigslist users from different parts of the country who regularly buy and sell secondhand items. Their feedback pointed to a few clear pain points:

  • Confusing login flow with redundant steps
  • Lack of guidance for first-time users
  • No quick or easy support when users get stuck

These insights helped me zero in on the key issues to address in the redesign.

From Problems to Prototypes

User Persona

After gathering insights from my interviews, I created a clear user persona to keep me focused on real user needs.

User Flow

Using this, I mapped a new sign-in flow that kept things simple while introducing meaningful personalization to help users feel more welcomed and confident as they got started.

Wireframes

I started with quick, low-fidelity wireframes to outline the key steps of the new onboarding experience.

From there, I moved swiftly into mid-fidelity versions, refining the layout and adding clearer copy to align the design with the final user experience.

High-Fidelity Mockups

Once the flow felt right, I focused on polishing the visuals. The goal was to give Craigslist’s outdated look a refresh while keeping it familiar. Every button and message was designed to feel clear, clean, and easy to follow.

Prototyping & Testing

With the design in place, I built an interactive prototype and ran remote tests with three users, swapping in a backup when one couldn’t make it.

Testing showed the new flow cut down on confusion and helped people sign in faster. It also highlighted users need for quicker help when stuck, so I implemented an AI chatbot to answer questions right away, instead of burying help in an FAQ.

What I Learned

  • Small changes can make a big difference: Streamlining onboarding helped reduce frustration and drop-offs faster than expected.
  • Personalization builds connection: Adding relevant prompts made users feel more comfortable and engaged right from sign-in.
  • Working solo sharpened my focus: Managing every step in one week taught me how to prioritize what really matters without getting lost in details.
  • Rapid testing uncovers surprises: User feedback revealed needs I hadn’t anticipated, like the desire for quick, easy support.
  • Design is an ongoing process: Even after this sprint, I see opportunities to refine and improve the experience based on real user behavior.
